Project Overview

Alvo is a FPS (First Person Shooter) VR game in Battle Royale mode - a group of players fight to the end, last survivor wins.

The goal was to push the edge of what's possible in VR, and make Alvo stand out among the sea of competition.

In fall of 2019, I was approached by the CEO of Mardonpol Inc. - a 2x client of mine - to design interface for a VR game.

In this case study, I'll walk you through how I morphed exisiting design principles to suit a medium I had never designed for before.

Reality Check

The first step was to research what users are already familiar with and expect a VR game to be. (Jacob's Law)

Unsurprisingly, VR Field of View (FOV) mimicks real life - player focus remains at the center of the FOV thus allowing anything to be placed within that area.

Real vs. reality

It comes with a catch, though - place elements too near and it will cause nausea, place them too far and it'd be difficult to see.

To take full advantage of the wider field of view in VR, it was important to have an idea where on the plane it was easiest for the player to interact.

Prompts to encourage exploration

I explored the idea of prompting users to turn their heads by placing signs and overlapping elements in the Field of View.

An outline view of how I viewed the gamer

There was also the question of simulating VR in Figma, a 2D software. I decided to create virtual scenes out of multiple images, and used a 1440x900 window as a viewport for the gamer.

It turned out to be a good find, as seen in the next section.

Indicators for additonal information

Turning heads, literally.

Putting my theories to the test, events outside the FOV triggered a marker that drew attention.

I took care, though, to put everything important within the gamer's HUD, creating a surreal experience not often seen in traditional gameplay.

The in-game HUD

Virtual POV

Even though this is a new medium, gamers shouldn't have to learn a new way of navigating it.

Taking a cue from existing games and traditional UX, I created an interface that was familiar for gamers and adapted to VR effortlessly.

  • In-game mode selection
  • In-game weapon choice

Spoilt for choice.

Just like a traditional Battle Royale, loadouts have been added that can be customised with different items and can be accessed in-game.

These are traditional menus that would appear hovering in "mid-air" and can be accessed using the VR controller.

A simulation of turning left and right

Victory

Using the same pattern of turning heads, gamers will be able to start a new game from the vitory screen itself.

Alvo has modes of gameplay with different maps, and an user would be able to see the details in a helpful tile.

Level up your game

Gamers won't be gamers if they didn't want to see their progress.

Alvo wouldn't be Alvo if they didn't reward them for it.

Military icons were used to create badges that were rewarded to players in recognition for their skill levels in the game.

Military Icons (courtesy: thenounproject)

In-game purchases

Games are fun, and players love to showcase individuality while having fun. Alvo didn't want them to miss out on that.

Several random pattern & color masks were created to give the players a choice in how they look like to their teammates, and how they show up in leaderboards.

  • Loadout to select weapons,
  • and customise their skins

Caring for the gamer

VR headsets have been said to create a feeling of nausea in certain cases - care has been taken not to include any flashing graphics or distracting animations that might accelerate the feeling.

Addtionally, rounds have been kept at a max limit of 10 mins and a virtual warning was included if the player is using the headset for a long time.

Outcome

The design was a successful implementation of the primary objective: make Alvo stand out in a sea of similar games

Developers found it very easy work with the style guide. The assets were custom made to export easily for Unity, which was the program of choice for the developers.

Project Learnings

  • Design fundamentals are universal.

    From VR to Web to apps, design fundamentals of UX never change and humans will never stop responding to good design.

  • Pushing the edge

    VR is a very challenging platform to design for, but pushing the edge is what makes it interesting.
